Skause recipe

Illustration by Gemini Nano Banana Pro

Description

Skause is a hearty, traditional Viking stew that perfectly reflects the resourcefulness and practicality of Norse cuisine. Originally designed to sustain Viking communities through harsh Scandinavian winters, long sea voyages, and after exhausting raids, this slow-cooked meal relied entirely on whatever ingredients were locally available or able to be preserved. It highlights the deeply agricultural and foraging roots of Viking society, prioritizing sustenance and warmth without sacrificing flavor.

The beauty of Skause lies in its adaptable simplicity. It historically featured meats like lamb alongside hardy root vegetables that could be stored for months, such as turnips, onions, and carrots. A unique hallmark of the dish is the addition of wild foraged berries—like lingonberries—which introduce a tart, fruity brightness that beautifully cuts through the rich, savory broth and heavy meats.

Ingredients

  • 1 lb lamb, cubed
  • 2 tbsp butter
  • 1 large onion, diced
  • 2 medium purple carrots, sliced
  • 2 medium turnips, diced
  • 1/2 head of red cabbage, chopped
  • 1 bay leaf
  • 1 tsp fresh thyme
  • 1/2 cup lingonberries (fresh or frozen)
  • Salt and pepper to taste
  • Water or broth to cover

Steps

  1. Brown the Lamb: Melt the butter in a large pot over medium heat. Add the cubed lamb and brown it on all sides to develop flavor.
  2. Add the Onions: Toss in the diced onion and cook until softened and slightly caramelized.
  3. Add the Root Vegetables: Stir in the sliced carrots and diced turnips.
  4. Add the Cabbage: Add the chopped red cabbage to the pot.
  5. Season and Simmer: Toss in the bay leaf, fresh thyme, and a generous pinch of salt and pepper. Pour in enough water or broth to just cover the ingredients. Bring the mixture to a boil, then reduce the heat, cover, and let it simmer gently for about 1.5 to 2 hours (or until the lamb and vegetables are completely tender).
  6. Finish with Lingonberries: During the last 10 minutes of cooking, stir in the lingonberries to add a tart contrast to the rich stew.
  7. Serve: Remove the bay leaf, ladle into bowls, and serve hot.
